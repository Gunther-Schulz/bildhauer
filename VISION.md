# Bildhauer — Vision

A Bildhauer starts with a rough block of stone and a vision. Not a blueprint — the final form can't be fully known before the work begins. The material reveals constraints as it's shaped.

But the sculptor doesn't start on the marble. They build a Bozzetto — a small clay model. Clay is cheap to reshape, easy to rework. The sculptor iterates on the Bozzetto: adjusting proportions, trying different poses, checking the gesture from every angle. Sometimes they make several, each more refined than the last. The refinement happens here, on the model, where mistakes cost nothing. Only when the model is right do they commit to the stone.

The refinement is continuous — each pass slightly finer than the last, always covering the whole piece. First you see basic masses: where the head is relative to the torso, where the limbs are, the overall stance. Then the arm separates from the torso, the face gets planes for forehead and cheek and chin. Then the hand gets individual fingers, the eyelid gets a curve. At any moment, the whole model looks like a rougher version of the final result. The sculptor just keeps refining, and the work gradually reveals more detail as it progresses.

Nothing is detailed in isolation. Every part is shaped in relation to every other part. The elbow matches the shoulder. The nose fits the face. At every level of resolution, the whole model is coherent — a complete figure, just at the resolution reached so far. It never looks like a detailed face on a featureless block.

At every stage, the sculptor steps back and looks. Not just at what they're working on — at the whole piece. They don't trust their hands; they verify with their eyes. They look from multiple angles. They measure and compare. This checking is not separate from the work — it is the work. Shape, step back, look. Shape, step back, look. The rhythm of stepping back emerges naturally from the work — sometimes after thirty seconds, sometimes after ten minutes. Depends on what the clay is telling them.

Sometimes during finer work, something feels off. A bump on the cheekbone. The sculptor could sand it down — a quick local fix. But instead they step back and check: is this just a bump, or is the whole jaw shifted? They compare both sides, look at the whole face. If it's just a bump, they sand it. If the jaw is shifted, the bump is a symptom — and before reshaping the jaw, they look at what's built on top of it. The cheekbone, the ear placement, the line of the neck — reshaping the jaw affects all of these. The sculptor checks both directions: what caused this problem, and what will fixing it affect.

There is another kind of stepping back — rarer but more consequential. The sculptor has been refining a figure with arms at its sides. The proportions are good, the structure is emerging. Then they stop and ask: should this figure have its arms at its sides at all? Maybe the gesture is wrong. Maybe this should be a figure in motion. This isn't checking proportions — it's questioning the vision itself. And this questioning extends to the commission too. The client asked for arms at sides, but the sculptor's expertise says the gesture would be stronger in motion, or the stone's grain suggests a different pose. The sculptor doesn't just execute — they surface what the client might not see. The client decides, but with the sculptor's perspective, not without it.

The sculptor doesn't question the vision every five minutes. But they do at transitions — when a section of work is complete and the next is about to begin. "Before I start on the arms, am I sure about the pose?" The cost of discovering the wrong pose after the arms are detailed is far greater than pausing to validate the vision now.

When the Bozzetto is refined enough — proportions right, structure coherent, every part in relation to the whole — the sculptor commits to the marble. The stone is cut following the model. The finest details (the texture of skin, the sharpness of an eyelid) are resolved during carving, because these genuinely can't be determined until chisel meets stone. But the coarse and medium decisions — the proportions, the structure, the relationships between parts — are all settled before the marble is touched.

If you tried to do it all at once — skip the Bozzetto, go straight to the marble, shape the block, define the structure, and polish the surface in a single pass — you'd get a mess. Fragments that are individually reasonable but don't relate to each other. The shoulders slightly uneven, and every detail built on them inherits the error. The further you go before catching it, the more work you lose when you finally have to go back. And on marble, going back is expensive.

Coherence is the sculptor's central discipline, but not their only one. They also read the grain of the stone — carving against it produces a surface that looks finished but fractures under stress. They reinforce thin parts that would snap in transport. They choose a block proportional to the work, not a ten-ton slab for a bust. They keep joints accessible so a future conservator can repair what time damages. They plan the base and mounting from the start, because a sculpture that can't be installed has failed regardless of its beauty. Each of these is attended to at its natural moment — the grain during every cut, the proportions at the start, the base throughout — not as a checklist run at the end.
